Dating Archaeological Sites

Understanding the past is impossible without being able to tell time. In order to examine how things have changed through time, archaeologists need to be able to find out when people lived at a site. This way, we have an understanding of the order in which things happened. Luckily, archaeologists and other scientists have developed a number of ways to date archaeological sites.

One common way archaeologists use to date archaeological sites is by tracking changes in artifact styles. Over time, people change the way objects look, such as shape, color, size, and many others. These changes can be used to say whether one site is older or younger than another. For example, below is an image of Coca-cola bottles through time.

Look at the ways that the shape of the bottle changed, as well as the material the bottles were made out of. Over time, Coke bottles changed to have a different shape (from straight sides to curved sides), and switched from being made of out glass to being made out of plastic or metal. Based on these changes, archaeologists can suggest an age for a site based on what types of Coke bottles are found there. For example, if you found a bunch of brown glass Coke bottles with straight sides at a site, but no other kinds of bottles, in what years do you think people lived at that site?

Stratigraphy

Another way that archaeologists tell time is through the principle of stratigraphy. The main idea behind stratigraphy is that older things will be buried below more recent things, making it possible for archaeologists to say generally that some types of objects or sites came before or after others. Think about your dirty cloths basket at home. On Monday night, you put your dirty cloths into the hamper. Then on Tuesday, you again throw your dirty cloths into the basket. By Friday, you have a basket full of cloths piled on top of each other. If you dug through this basket to find something, your cloths from Monday would on the bottom, Tuesday's cloths would be on top of those, Wednesday's cloths would be on top of Tuesday's, on and on and on. Stratigraphy works in the same way. If you have a house that was built on top of the ruins of a second house, then you know that the house built on top is generally more recent then the house it was built upon.
