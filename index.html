What is Archaeology?

So what is archaeology anyways? Archaeology is the study of past people through objects they have left behind. In many parts of the world, people who lived a long, long time ago did not write down everything they did or take pictures of people around them. Instead, the only things we have left of them are crumbling buildings and trash from their every day lives. Luckily, using these objects, archaeologists can learn many things about past people. Archaeology, in many ways, is like a large detective story, where we look at all of the clues left behind in order to create a story about what happened in a particular place at a certain time.

One way that archaeologists gather these clues is by digging very slowly and carefully in the ground to collect objects made by past people (called artifacts) or to find the remains of houses or other important buildings. This is called excavation. It is a dirty job, but it can be a lot of fun and is a great way to uncover clues about the past.

About This Website

If you would like to learn more about archaeology and what archaeologists do, click on the buttons at the top of the webpage. These are titled "Time," "Place," and "Artifacts." Each button will take you to a new page that will teach you about one aspect of archaeology, such as how archaeologists tell how old something is or what different artifacts can tell you about the past. If you would like to explore other activities or websites related to archaeology, click on the "Resources" button to find a list of other options.

ABOUT THE PROJECT

Archaeology 101 is an interactive web-based teaching aid designed to introduce primary school students to archaeology and the study of the past. It was developed as part of the Cultural Heritage Informatics Fellwoship at Michigan State University.
